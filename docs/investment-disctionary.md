# Investing Jargon (One-Sentence Definitions with Resources)

- **Alpha** – The amount an investment outperforms or underperforms its benchmark after adjusting for risk. [Learn more](https://www.investopedia.com/terms/a/alpha.asp)
- **Asset** – Anything of value that can be owned and potentially generate cash flow, such as stocks, bonds, or real estate.
- **Asset Allocation** – The way an investor splits money among different asset classes like stocks, bonds, and cash.
- **Bear Market** – A prolonged period when prices fall significantly and pessimism dominates the market. [Learn more](https://www.investopedia.com/terms/b/bearmarket.asp)
- **Bull Market** – A prolonged period when prices rise significantly and optimism dominates the market. [Learn more](https://www.investopedia.com/terms/b/bullmarket.asp)
- **Benchmark** – A standard index or portfolio used to compare an investment's performance. [Learn more](https://www.investopedia.com/terms/b/benchmark.asp)
- **Beta** – A measure of how much an investment's price moves relative to the overall market's movements. [Learn more](https://www.investopedia.com/terms/b/beta.asp)
- **Bid Price** – The highest price a buyer is currently willing to pay for a security.
- **Ask Price** – The lowest price a seller is currently willing to accept for a security.
- **Bid‑Ask Spread** – The difference between the bid and ask prices, often reflecting liquidity and trading costs. [Learn more](https://www.investopedia.com/terms/b/bid-askspread.asp)
- **Blue‑Chip Stock** – Shares of large, financially stable, well‑known companies with long records of reliable performance. [Learn more](https://www.investopedia.com/terms/b/bluechip.asp)
- **Bond** – A loan investors give to governments or companies in exchange for regular interest payments and repayment at maturity. [Learn more](https://www.investopedia.com/terms/b/bond.asp)
- **Book Value** – The net value of a company's assets minus its liabilities as recorded on the balance sheet. [Learn more](https://www.investopedia.com/terms/b/bookvalue.asp)
- **Broker** – A licensed intermediary that executes buy and sell orders for investors, usually charging a fee or commission. [Learn more](https://www.investopedia.com/terms/b/broker.asp)
- **Capital** – Money or assets that can be used to invest, operate a business, or generate more money.
- **Capital Gain** – The profit made when an asset is sold for more than its purchase price. [Learn more](https://www.investopedia.com/terms/c/capitalgain.asp)
- **Capital Loss** – The loss incurred when an asset is sold for less than its purchase price. [Learn more](https://www.investopedia.com/terms/c/capitalloss.asp)
- **Capital Markets** – Markets where long‑term securities like stocks and bonds are issued and traded. [Learn more](https://www.investopedia.com/terms/c/capitalmarkets.asp)
- **Cash Flow** – The net amount of cash moving into and out of a business or investment over a period of time. [Learn more](https://www.investopedia.com/terms/c/cashflow.asp)
- **Commission** – A fee paid to a broker for executing a buy or sell order. [Learn more](https://www.investopedia.com/terms/c/commission.asp)
- **Compound Interest** – Interest earned on both the original principal and on previously accumulated interest. [Learn more](https://www.investopedia.com/terms/c/compoundinterest.asp)
- **Correction** – A temporary price decline of roughly ten percent or more from a recent high in a market or asset. [Learn more](https://www.investopedia.com/terms/c/correction.asp)
- **Correlation** – A statistical measure of how closely two investments move in relation to each other. [Learn more](https://www.investopedia.com/terms/c/correlation.asp)
- **Coupon (Bond)** – The regular interest payment a bondholder receives, usually expressed as a percentage of the bond's face value. [Learn more](https://www.investopedia.com/terms/c/coupon.asp)
- **Current Ratio** – A liquidity metric comparing a company's current assets to its current liabilities. [Learn more](https://www.investopedia.com/terms/c/currentratio.asp)
- **Custodian** – A financial institution that holds securities on behalf of investors for safekeeping. [Learn more](https://www.investopedia.com/terms/c/custodian.asp)
- **Day Trading** – Buying and selling financial instruments within the same trading day to profit from short‑term price moves. [Learn more](https://www.investopedia.com/terms/d/daytrader.asp)
- **Debt‑to‑Equity Ratio** – A measure showing how much a company is financed by debt compared with shareholders' equity. [Learn more](https://www.investopedia.com/terms/d/debtequityratio.asp)
- **Default** – Failure of a borrower to make required interest or principal payments on time. [Learn more](https://www.investopedia.com/terms/d/default2.asp)
- **Delta (Options)** – A measure of how much an option's price changes when the underlying asset's price changes slightly. [Learn more](https://www.investopedia.com/terms/d/delta.asp)
- **Derivatives** – Financial contracts whose value is based on an underlying asset, index, rate, or event. [Learn more](https://www.investopedia.com/terms/d/derivative.asp)
- **Diversification** – Spreading investments across different assets to reduce the impact of any single loss. [Learn more](https://www.investopedia.com/terms/d/diversification.asp)
- **Dividend** – A cash or stock payment a company makes to its shareholders from profits or reserves. [Learn more](https://www.investopedia.com/terms/d/dividend.asp)
- **Dividend Yield** – The annual dividends per share divided by the stock's current price, shown as a percentage. [Learn more](https://www.investopedia.com/terms/d/dividendyield.asp)
- **Drawdown** – The percentage decline from a portfolio's or asset's peak value to its subsequent low point. [Learn more](https://www.investopedia.com/terms/d/drawdown.asp)
- **Earnings** – A company's profits after expenses, often reported each quarter and year. [Learn more](https://www.investopedia.com/terms/e/earnings.asp)
- **Earnings Per Share (EPS)** – A company's profit divided by the number of outstanding shares. [Learn more](https://www.investopedia.com/terms/e/eps.asp)
- **Emerging Markets** – Developing countries' markets that offer faster growth but usually higher risk and volatility. [Learn more](https://www.investopedia.com/terms/e/emergingmarketeconomy.asp)
- **Equities** – Another term for stocks, representing ownership shares in a company. [Learn more](https://www.investopedia.com/terms/e/equity.asp)
- **ETF (Exchange‑Traded Fund)** – A fund that holds a basket of assets and trades on an exchange like a stock. [Learn more](https://www.investopedia.com/terms/e/etf.asp)
- **Face Value (Par Value)** – The amount a bond will pay back at maturity, or the nominal value printed on a security. [Learn more](https://www.investopedia.com/terms/f/facevalue.asp)
- **Fiduciary** – A person or firm legally obligated to act in the best financial interest of a client. [Learn more](https://www.investopedia.com/terms/f/fiduciary.asp)
- **Float** – The number of a company's shares that are available for public trading. [Learn more](https://www.investopedia.com/terms/f/float.asp)
- **Fundamental Analysis** – Evaluating investments by examining financial statements, business models, and economic factors. [Learn more](https://www.investopedia.com/terms/f/fundamentalanalysis.asp)
- **Futures Contract** – A standardized agreement to buy or sell an asset at a set price on a future date. [Learn more](https://www.investopedia.com/terms/f/futurescontract.asp)
- **Growth Stock** – A company share expected to grow earnings faster than the market average, often reinvesting profits instead of paying dividends. [Learn more](https://www.investopedia.com/terms/g/growthstock.asp)
- **Hedge** – An investment or strategy used to reduce or offset the risk of another position. [Learn more](https://www.investopedia.com/terms/h/hedge.asp)
- **Hedge Fund** – A pooled investment vehicle that uses complex strategies and often leverage, typically for accredited investors. [Learn more](https://www.investopedia.com/terms/h/hedgefund.asp)
- **High‑Yield Bond (Junk Bond)** – A bond with lower credit quality that pays higher interest to compensate for greater risk. [Learn more](https://www.investopedia.com/terms/h/high_yield_bond.asp)
- **Illiquid** – Describes an asset that is hard to quickly buy or sell without significantly affecting its price. [Learn more](https://www.investopedia.com/terms/i/illiquid.asp)
- **Index** – A statistical measure that tracks the performance of a group of securities, like a market segment. [Learn more](https://www.investopedia.com/terms/i/index.asp)
- **Index Fund** – A fund designed to replicate the performance of a specific market index. [Learn more](https://www.investopedia.com/terms/i/indexfund.asp)
- **Inflation** – The rate at which general prices for goods and services rise, eroding purchasing power over time. [Learn more](https://www.investopedia.com/terms/i/inflation.asp)
- **Initial Public Offering (IPO)** – The first time a private company offers its shares to the public on a stock exchange. [Learn more](https://www.investopedia.com/terms/i/ipo.asp)
- **Intrinsic Value** – An estimate of what an asset is truly worth based on fundamentals, regardless of its current market price. [Learn more](https://www.investopedia.com/terms/i/intrinsicvalue.asp)
- **Leverage** – Using borrowed money or derivatives to increase the potential return (and risk) of an investment. [Learn more](https://www.investopedia.com/terms/l/leverage.asp)
- **Liability** – A financial obligation or debt a person or company owes to others. [Learn more](https://www.investopedia.com/terms/l/liability.asp)
- **Limit Order** – An order to buy or sell a security only at a specific price or better. [Learn more](https://www.investopedia.com/terms/l/limitorder.asp)
- **Liquidity** – How easily and quickly an asset can be converted into cash without major price impact. [Learn more](https://www.investopedia.com/terms/l/liquidity.asp)
- **Margin** – Borrowed money from a broker used to buy securities, with the securities themselves serving as collateral. [Learn more](https://www.investopedia.com/terms/m/margin.asp)
- **Margin Call** – A broker's demand for an investor to deposit more cash or securities to meet minimum equity requirements. [Learn more](https://www.investopedia.com/terms/m/margincall.asp)
- **Market Capitalization (Market Cap)** – The total value of a company's outstanding shares, calculated as share price times share count. [Learn more](https://www.investopedia.com/terms/m/marketcapitalization.asp)
- **Market Maker** – A firm that continuously quotes buy and sell prices and stands ready to trade, helping provide liquidity. [Learn more](https://www.investopedia.com/terms/m/marketmaker.asp)
- **Market Order** – An order to buy or sell a security immediately at the best available current price. [Learn more](https://www.investopedia.com/terms/m/marketorder.asp)
- **Market Volatility** – The degree of variation in asset prices over time, often reflecting uncertainty or risk. [Learn more](https://www.investopedia.com/terms/v/volatility.asp)
- **Momentum** – A strategy or concept where assets that have been rising or falling tend to continue in the same direction for a time. [Learn more](https://www.investopedia.com/terms/m/momentum.asp)
- **Mutual Fund** – A pooled investment vehicle managed by professionals that buys a diversified portfolio of securities. [Learn more](https://www.investopedia.com/terms/m/mutualfund.asp)
- **Net Asset Value (NAV)** – The value per share of a fund, calculated as total assets minus liabilities divided by shares outstanding. [Learn more](https://www.investopedia.com/terms/n/nav.asp)
- **Over‑the‑Counter (OTC)** – Trading securities directly between parties rather than through a centralized exchange. [Learn more](https://www.investopedia.com/terms/o/otc.asp)
- **Overvalued** – Describes an asset believed to be priced higher than its fundamental worth. [Learn more](https://www.investopedia.com/terms/o/overvalued.asp)
- **Passive Investing** – An approach that aims to match market returns, often by tracking indexes, instead of trying to beat them. [Learn more](https://www.investopedia.com/terms/p/passiveinvesting.asp)
- **Penny Stock** – A very low‑priced, often thinly traded stock considered highly speculative and risky. [Learn more](https://www.investopedia.com/terms/p/pennystock.asp)
- **Portfolio** – The collection of all investments owned by an individual or institution. [Learn more](https://www.investopedia.com/terms/p/portfolio.asp)
- **Price‑to‑Book Ratio (P/B)** – A valuation measure comparing a stock's market price to its book value per share. [Learn more](https://www.investopedia.com/terms/p/price-to-bookratio.asp)
- **Price‑to‑Earnings Ratio (P/E)** – A valuation measure comparing a stock's current price to its earnings per share. [Learn more](https://www.investopedia.com/terms/p/price-earningsratio.asp)
- **Private Equity** – Investments in private companies or buyouts of public companies, usually with active management involvement. [Learn more](https://www.investopedia.com/terms/p/privateequity.asp)
- **Prospectus** – A formal document describing an investment offering's details, risks, and terms. [Learn more](https://www.investopedia.com/terms/p/prospectus.asp)
- **Quantitative Easing (QE)** – A central bank policy of creating money to buy financial assets and stimulate the economy. [Learn more](https://www.investopedia.com/terms/q/quantitativeeasing.asp)
- **Real Return** – An investment's return after adjusting for the effects of inflation. [Learn more](https://www.investopedia.com/terms/r/realreturn.asp)
- **Rebalancing** – Adjusting a portfolio's holdings to bring asset weights back to desired target allocations. [Learn more](https://www.investopedia.com/terms/r/rebalancing.asp)
- **Return on Equity (ROE)** – A profitability metric showing how effectively a company generates profit from shareholders' equity. [Learn more](https://www.investopedia.com/terms/r/returnonequity.asp)
- **Risk Tolerance** – The amount of investment loss or volatility an investor is psychologically and financially able to accept. [Learn more](https://www.investopedia.com/terms/r/risktolerance.asp)
- **Risk‑Adjusted Return** – A measure of how much return an investment generates relative to the risk taken. [Learn more](https://www.investopedia.com/terms/r/risk-adjustedreturn.asp)
- **Russell 2000** – An index tracking small‑cap US companies, widely used as a benchmark for small‑cap performance. [Learn more](https://www.investopedia.com/terms/r/russell2000.asp)
- **S\&P 500** – An index that tracks 500 large‑cap US companies and serves as a broad market benchmark. [Learn more](https://www.investopedia.com/terms/s/sp500.asp)
- **Nasdaq** – An index tracking primarily technology and growth companies, often used as a tech sector benchmark. [Learn more](https://www.investopedia.com/terms/n/nasdaq.asp)
- **Dow Jones Industrial Average** – An index tracking 30 large‑cap blue‑chip US companies. [Learn more](https://www.investopedia.com/terms/d/djia.asp)
- **Secondary Market** – The market where existing securities are traded between investors after their initial issuance. [Learn more](https://www.investopedia.com/terms/s/secondarymarket.asp)
- **Sector** – A group of companies that operate in the same part of the economy, such as technology or healthcare. [Learn more](https://www.investopedia.com/terms/s/sector.asp)
- **Sharpe Ratio** – A metric that shows how much excess return an investment delivers per unit of risk or volatility. [Learn more](https://www.investopedia.com/terms/s/sharperatio.asp)
- **Short Selling (Shorting)** – Borrowing a security to sell it now, hoping to buy it back later at a lower price for profit. [Learn more](https://www.investopedia.com/terms/s/shortselling.asp)
- **Spread (Credit or Yield)** – The difference in yield between two bonds or instruments, often indicating relative risk. [Learn more](https://www.investopedia.com/terms/s/spread.asp)
- **Stop‑Loss Order** – An order that automatically sells a security if its price falls to a specified level to limit losses. [Learn more](https://www.investopedia.com/terms/s/stoplosorder.asp)
- **Style Drift** – When a fund or manager deviates from its stated investment style or mandate.
- **Systematic Risk** – Market‑wide risk that cannot be eliminated by diversification, such as recessions or interest‑rate shocks. [Learn more](https://www.investopedia.com/terms/s/systematicrisk.asp)
- **Tail Risk** – The risk of rare but extreme events that cause very large losses. [Learn more](https://www.investopedia.com/terms/t/tailrisk.asp)
- **Technical Analysis** – Evaluating investments by analyzing price charts, patterns, and trading volume rather than fundamentals. [Learn more](https://www.investopedia.com/terms/t/technicalanalysis.asp)
- **Time Horizon** – The length of time an investor expects to hold investments before needing the money. [Learn more](https://www.investopedia.com/terms/t/timehorizon.asp)
- **Total Return** – The combination of price changes and income (like dividends or interest) from an investment. [Learn more](https://www.investopedia.com/terms/t/totalreturn.asp)
- **Treasuries** – Debt securities issued by a national government, generally considered very low risk in that government's currency. [Learn more](https://www.investopedia.com/terms/t/treasurybond.asp)
- **Underperform** – To earn a lower return than a benchmark or comparable investments. [Learn more](https://www.investopedia.com/terms/u/underperform.asp)
- **Undervalued** – Describes an asset believed to be priced lower than its fundamental worth. [Learn more](https://www.investopedia.com/terms/u/undervalued.asp)
- **Volatility** – The degree of fluctuation in an asset's price over time, often used as a proxy for risk. [Learn more](https://www.investopedia.com/terms/v/volatility.asp)
- **Yield** – The income an investment generates over a period, usually expressed as a percentage of its price or value. [Learn more](https://investor.vanguard.com/investor-resources-education/article/bond-yields-explained)
- **Yield Curve** – A graph that shows interest rates for bonds of the same credit quality but different maturities. [Learn more](https://www.investopedia.com/terms/y/yieldcurve.asp)
- **Zero‑Coupon Bond** – A bond that pays no periodic interest and is issued at a discount, returning its full face value at maturity. [Learn more](https://www.investopedia.com/terms/z/zero-couponbond.asp)
